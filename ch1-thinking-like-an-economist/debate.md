# Policy Debate: Should University Education Be Free?

**Chapter 1 Model**: Opportunity cost, scarcity, production possibilities frontier
**Difficulty**: Introductory -- no advanced economics needed

## The Question

> Several European countries (Germany, Norway, Finland) offer tuition-free university education funded by taxpayers. Cyprus charges tuition at public universities (around EUR 3,400/year at UCY). The US charges significantly more (average $10,000+ at public universities). Should Cyprus adopt a tuition-free model?

---

## Before You Argue: Key Data

- Cyprus public university tuition: ~EUR 3,400/year
- Cyprus GDP: ~EUR 28 billion (2023)
- Cyprus government education spending: 5.5% of GDP (~EUR 1.54 billion)
- University enrollment: ~23,000 students in public universities
- Youth unemployment (15-24): ~17%
- Germany abolished tuition in 2014; enrollment increased 25% within 5 years
- Finland's free university system rejects ~70% of applicants (selective)

*Use the SAME concepts to argue the opposite. This is harder -- and more important.*

### Your argument (fill in):

**What does the PPF framework say?**
[Free university isn't free -- the money comes from taxes. What other things could the government fund with the same money? What's the opportunity cost of education spending?]

**What is the opportunity cost of making it free?**
[If the government spends EUR X million more on universities, that's EUR X million NOT spent on hospitals, infrastructure, defense, or tax cuts. Who bears this cost?]

**What does the model miss?**
[Does "free" change who attends? Finland's free system is highly selective. Germany's is not. Do they have the same outcomes? What if "free" leads to students enrolling who aren't committed, wasting resources?]

## What Economists Actually Think

The professional consensus is nuanced:
- Most economists agree education has **positive externalities** (you'll learn this term in Chapter 14) -- society benefits beyond just the individual student
- The debate is about **mechanism**, not **goal**: subsidies? income-based repayment? free for all? free only for low-income?
- Evidence from natural experiments (Germany 2014, England's tripling of fees in 2012) suggests price affects WHO attends more than WHETHER people attend
- The equity argument depends heavily on WHO benefits from free tuition -- if mostly upper-middle-class students attend university, free tuition is a regressive subsidy
