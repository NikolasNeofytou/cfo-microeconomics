## What You Just Experienced

You discovered that "free" university costs EUR 21,200/year, that Instagram costs 182 hours of your life annually, and that every government spending decision means something else doesn't get funded. The common thread: **every choice has a cost, and that cost is what you give up.**

Chapter 1 gives you the formal framework for thinking this way about *everything*.

## Section Priorities

### Study Carefully

- **Why Study Economics?**: Notice how the authors frame economics not as "the study of money" but as "the study of choice under scarcity." This distinction matters. Every scenario from the puzzle was about choice, not money.

- **The Scope of Economics / Microeconomics vs Macroeconomics**: You're studying micro -- individual decisions, markets, prices. Macro is aggregates (GDP, inflation, unemployment). Understand the boundary because Case/Fair/Oster will occasionally reference macro concepts.

- **The Method of Economics / Economic Models**: Pay close attention to what a "model" is. A model is a SIMPLIFIED representation of reality. The PPF is a model. Supply and demand is a model. They're useful precisely BECAUSE they leave things out. The question is always: does the simplification help us understand, or does it hide something important?

- **Opportunity Cost**: This is the single most important concept in the entire textbook. Every chapter builds on it. Make sure you can explain it in your own words and apply it to a new example.

- **The Production Possibilities Frontier**: This is the first economic model you'll learn. Understand: (1) why the frontier is bowed outward (increasing opportunity cost), (2) what it means to be ON the frontier vs INSIDE it, (3) what shifts the frontier outward.

### Skim for Context

- **Economic Policy / Why Economists Disagree**: Good context for Phase 4 (debate), but the analytical tools are in the sections above. Skim for the positive vs normative economics distinction -- you'll use it in the debate phase.

- **Ceteris Paribus**: "All else equal." This assumption appears in EVERY model this textbook builds. Note it, understand why it's necessary, and start noticing when it's unrealistic.

## Questions While Reading

### MIT-style (analytical)
1. Draw a PPF for a country that produces food and computers. If the country is currently producing at a point inside the frontier, what does this tell you about its resource utilization?

2. Calculate: if producing one more unit of computers requires giving up 3 units of food, what is the opportunity cost of one computer? What is the opportunity cost of one unit of food?

### Harvard-style (intuition-first)
3. Before reading about the PPF shape, predict: as a country produces more and more computers (and less food), does each additional computer cost the same amount of food, more food, or less food? Why?

4. Think of a decision you made this week. What was the opportunity cost? Include both monetary and non-monetary costs.

### Yale-style (critical thinking)
5. The PPF model assumes only two goods. Real economies produce millions of goods. Does this make the model useless, or is the simplification justified? When would a two-good model mislead you?

6. The text says resources are "scarce." But we throw away 30-40% of food produced globally. If resources are wasted, are they really scarce? How does the PPF framework handle waste?
